Case Study · Social Sector AI

Grant Intelligence System

A production AI system for a tribal rights NGO managing multi-crore grants from major international foundations

Deployed · In Production Node.js RAG Pipeline GPT-4o NGO Finance Grant Compliance Social Sector

Context

VAAGDHARA has worked in tribal communities across Rajasthan, Gujarat, and Madhya Pradesh for decades — on land rights, natural farming, women's empowerment, and tribal governance. Much of that work is funded through grants from international foundations including Ford Foundation, MacArthur Foundation, Rainmatter, MISEREOR Germany, and Bread for the World.

The organization had no dedicated grants team. The people who understood the work best — program officers and leadership — were also the ones writing the proposals, on top of their actual field responsibilities.

The Problem

Every year, the team spent weeks on tasks that had almost nothing to do with the actual program work: searching for funders across websites, reading lengthy guidelines, checking eligibility, writing the same organizational background in different formats for different foundations.

The harder problem was invisible. Because this work was so time-consuming, the team could only pursue a small number of grants at once. Opportunities with good fit but tight timelines were often missed simply because there was not enough capacity to respond.

And even when a proposal was submitted, there was no institutional memory. What language had resonated with Ford Foundation. What framing had failed with another funder. Every proposal started from scratch, with no learning carried forward.

This was not a writing problem. It was an information problem with an economic structure: the cost of searching was too high, the cost of losing institutional memory was invisible, and the cost of missed opportunities was felt but never measured.

What I Built

A full-stack AI system with five integrated modules, deployed on Render with Google OAuth, production auth, and a file-based session store. The system is indexed against VAAGDHARA's real grant history.

  • Grant Scout Agent — runs 5 parallel live web searches, synthesizes with GPT-4o, returns a ranked list of funding opportunities with urgency ratings (hot/warm/cool) and GO/NO-GO recommendations with specific reasoning for each.
  • Funder Intelligence Engine — deep-crawls a funder's website: about pages, grant guidelines, leadership, annual reports. Extracts language patterns, power words, geographies of focus, and grant size ranges. Builds a structured "funder DNA" profile with VAAGDHARA-specific alignment analysis.
  • Concept Ideation Engine — multi-stage: Stage A generates 5 candidate proposal concepts using angle triangulation (funder priority × VAAGDHARA program × moment); Stage B refines to 3 with fit + diversity scoring, enriched with live web context on what the funder has recently funded.
  • Budget Strategist — GPT-4o powered, returns 3 budget scenarios (conservative/base/stretch) with component breakdowns, compliance flags, and overhead defence arguments tailored to the specific funder's policies.
  • RAG Service — custom vector store indexed against VAAGDHARA's real grant documents: MOUs, program reports, audited statements. Cosine similarity search with contextual injection into every proposal generation. The system knows what the organization has actually done, not just what it says it does.
  • Proposal Writer + DOCX Export — generates full proposal sections with RAG context injection. Exports formatted Word documents ready for final review. Includes a logic framework builder, CRM for meeting notes, and funder memory across sessions.

The system was also built with a real-world constraint baked in: VAAGDHARA will not accept funding from tobacco, mining, or gambling companies. That refusal logic is embedded in every search and evaluation prompt.

The Detail Worth Knowing

The hardest thing to get right was fit scoring. When the system evaluates a funding opportunity, it needs to give a real answer — not a generic "this could be relevant" — about whether this particular organization should apply.

Getting that right required encoding actual grant logic. Not just: does the organization's geography match? But: is this organization registered to receive foreign funding under FCRA? Does the funder's typical grant size match the organization's capacity to report and manage? Has the funder supported tribal rights specifically before, or only "rural development" broadly?

Some of that logic required understanding the NGO funding landscape in India — the FCRA constraints, how large foundations distinguish between program and operating grants, how EcoVadis-aligned funders think differently from faith-based foundations. The technology did not provide that. Economic knowledge of how the grants market works did.

Outcome

The system is running in production. The grants team can evaluate significantly more opportunities in the same amount of time, with institutional memory from previous applications carried forward automatically. The team can focus on what they know best — the field work — rather than the administrative overhead of identifying and applying for grants.

The system was given to the organization at no cost. That was the right decision. It is also meaningful proof of what this kind of system can do — built with 40+ API endpoints, five AI agents, real document indexing, and production deployment.

What I would do differently

I would have spent more time with the grants team before building anything. I understood the problem correctly, but I built some features that the team found less useful than expected — and missed some small things that would have made a real difference daily. The gap was not in the architecture. It was in not asking simpler questions earlier: "What do you do in the first hour of your morning when you sit down to work on a grant?" That question would have changed three design decisions.
