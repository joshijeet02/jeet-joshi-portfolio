Case Study · Government Data · Public Policy

Rajasthan Employment Dashboard

15 years of MNREGA employment data across 33 districts, built for a government policy briefing

Deployed React + TypeScript Data Engineering Public Policy Economic Analysis Government MIS

Context

MNREGA — the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act — is India's largest public works programme, guaranteeing 100 days of employment per year to rural households. It generates enormous data: persondays worked, households employed, wages paid, district breakdowns, across every financial year since 2008.

That data is publicly available on the government's MIS portal. But it is almost impossible to use analytically. Each financial year lives at a different endpoint, formatted differently, with inconsistent field names. Comparing performance across years or political tenures requires stitching together dozens of separate data pulls manually.

This dashboard was built for a chief-ministerial policy briefing — a context where the person asking questions has 15 minutes, not 15 hours.

The Core Design Challenge

Higher MNREGA numbers are not automatically better. High persondays can mean strong implementation — or severe drought, a pandemic, or crop failure. The dashboard had to show scale AND quality, always together.

Most data dashboards answer "what happened?" This one was designed to answer a different question: whether employment actually improved, rather than simply increasing. Those are not the same thing, and the difference is politically and economically significant.

That framing required economic thinking — knowing that rural employment data is counter-cyclical, that district-level patterns can contradict state averages, and that tenure comparisons need to account for transition years rather than treating raw averages as clean comparisons.

What I Built

  • Multi-source MIS scraping pipeline — pulls from three different government endpoint families (legacy demregister, live employment status, district financial statements) and normalizes into a single continuous data spine from FY2008–09 to FY2025–26.
  • Tenure comparison engine — identifies financial years that fall cleanly inside each political tenure and computes averages, while isolating transition years where comparison would be misleading.
  • District drill-down — block-level data for all 33 districts, with top/bottom district rankings for key indicators.
  • Finance layer — wage vs. material vs. admin expenditure breakdown, utilization rate, pending payment tracking.
  • React + TypeScript interactive dashboard — six tabs: Overview, Tenure Comparison, Districts, Finance, Works & Outcomes, Methodology. Maximum 6 headline metric cards on the home screen.
  • NotebookLM export bundle — structured CSVs and JSONs with a one-line prompt designed to auto-generate a presentation slide deck from the data.

The Economic Judgment Calls

Several design decisions required economic reasoning the data alone could not provide.

Which metrics to show first: Not raw numbers — normalized indicators. Average days per household (quality) alongside total persondays (scale). Women's share of persondays. Pending payments as an implementation quality signal, not just volume.

How to handle transition years: FY2023–24 straddles two state governments. Including it in either tenure's average distorts the comparison. It is shown in the trend line but flagged, not averaged into either tenure block.

What to leave off: Daily averages, GP-level detail, composite scores. A chief-ministerial briefing needs three clear answers, not a data warehouse. The dashboard was designed with an explicit list of metrics to exclude from the home screen.

Outcome

The dashboard is deployed and usable as a standalone analytical tool. The data pipeline is automated — pulling and normalizing current-year data as the MIS portal updates. The NotebookLM bundle was used to generate a presentation deck directly from the processed data.

More broadly: this project demonstrates what it looks like to build a data tool that encodes economic judgment — not just technical skill. The value goes beyond the scraping or the React code. It is in knowing which questions to ask and how to structure the data to answer them.

What I would do differently

I would have validated the district-level endpoints earlier. Some district-year combinations return server-side errors from the government MIS — not data gaps, but infrastructure failures. Building a more robust fallback system for missing district data from the start would have saved significant time.
