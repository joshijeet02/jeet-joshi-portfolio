VAAGDHARA Voice Agent & Knowledge Base

A Hindi-first knowledge system and voice AI for tribal women in Rajasthan, Gujarat, and Madhya Pradesh

Pilot-Ready Hindi NLPVoice AIRAG Community DevelopmentSocial Sector

Context

VAAGDHARA works with Bhil tribal communities across three states — primarily women — on land rights, food security, natural farming, and government welfare entitlements. Their frontline workers (sakhis) have direct conversations with women in villages about what they are entitled to, what schemes exist, and what to do when systems fail them.

The sakhis need accurate, culturally grounded information during live conversations. The knowledge existed — in organizational newsletters, program documents, field reports — but it was scattered across PDFs nobody in the field could navigate in the moment.

The Problem

This was not a technology problem. It was a knowledge access problem with a very specific set of constraints. The system had to work in Hindi. It had to speak the language of tribal communities — the actual names of government schemes as used in Banswara, the specific crops of Bhil agriculture, the cultural register that makes a conversation feel safe.

Generic AI tools — even those fine-tuned on Hindi — do not encode this kind of contextual knowledge. They know what MGNREGA is. They do not know how Bhil communities refer to it, what common payment delays look like in Banswara district, or how to discuss menstrual health with a woman who has never spoken about it to any official before.

The knowledge had to come from VAAGDHARA itself — from their two decades of field work — not from a generic training corpus. That is what made this fundamentally different from deploying an off-the-shelf chatbot.

What I Built

  • Structured Hindi knowledge base — extracted from VAAGDHARA's primary source documents. Organized into four intelligence domains: agricultural knowledge, government scheme entitlements, health and nutrition, and women's rights and legal protections.
  • 200-question scored dialogue bank — questions a frontline worker might ask, or a woman might raise. Each question scored by domain, emotional register (informational, distressed, aspirational), and likely follow-up. Gives a voice agent enough context to respond accurately and with care.
  • OCR pipeline for Hindi newsletters — VAAGDHARA's most valuable knowledge was in printed newsletters scanned as images. Built a Tesseract-based Hindi OCR pipeline followed by quality review flagging low-confidence extractions for manual correction.
  • Pilot-ready system prompt — a complete voice agent system prompt for platforms like Bland AI or ElevenLabs Conversational AI. Encodes the agent's persona, knowledge sources, language register, cultural sensitivity guidelines, and escalation paths for when to say "I don't know."
  • Knowledge gap report — identified 34 terms, local practices, and scheme variations that appeared in field interviews but were absent from existing documentation, flagged for VAAGDHARA's team to fill before launch.

The Detail Worth Knowing

The hardest design decision was about what the agent should not do. Generic AI, when uncertain, often gives a vague approximation. In a community development context — where a woman might rely on what she hears to decide whether to report a payment delay or access a health scheme — a confident wrong answer is actively harmful.

The system prompt was written with explicit refusal logic: when confidence is below a threshold, the agent says it does not know and refers to a human worker. This is a design choice about what kind of AI is appropriate in this setting. Knowing when not to answer is as important as knowing when to answer. That judgment required understanding the development sector context, not just the NLP task.

Outcome

The system is pilot-ready. Knowledge base, dialogue bank, and system prompt have been delivered and are ready for integration with a voice platform. The knowledge gap report identifies what needs to be documented before deployment.

The broader significance: this is what AI genuinely built for a community looks like, as opposed to AI built for a general user and then applied to a community. The difference is not in the technology. It is in where the knowledge comes from and whose context the system was designed around.

What I would do differently

I would have spent time in the field before building anything. I built the knowledge base from documents — the right starting point — but the most important contextual knowledge does not live in documents. It lives in the conversations sakhis have been having for years. One afternoon with two frontline workers would have significantly changed the system prompt's emotional register and added questions to the dialogue bank I could not have known to include.
